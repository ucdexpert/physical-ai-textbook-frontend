## Overview
This chapter provides a theoretical foundation for the entire course by diving into the fundamentals of Embodied Intelligence (or Embodied Cognition). This theory argues that an agent's intelligence is not just a product of its "brain" or software, but is deeply shaped by the characteristics of its physical body and its interactions with the environment. We will move beyond the simple sensor-motor loop and explore how the body itself can offload computational work‚Äîa concept known as morphological computation.

We will discuss the "grounding problem," a classic challenge in AI that questions how symbolic systems (like language models) can truly understand the meaning of the words they manipulate without having physical experience. Embodiment offers a solution by "grounding" concepts in sensory and motor experiences. For example, a robot understands the word "cup" not just by its dictionary definition but by seeing it, touching it, and learning to grasp it. We will contrast the challenges faced by embodied agents (like dealing with physical uncertainty and the "reality gap" between simulation and the real world) with those of purely digital AIs, setting the stage for the simulation and control techniques to come.

## Key Concepts
- **Morphological Computation**: The idea that the physical structure of a body can perform computations or simplify control tasks, reducing the burden on the central controller (brain).
- **Sensorimotor Contingencies**: The lawful patterns of change in sensory input that are caused by an agent's own actions. Learning these patterns is key to perception.
- **Grounding Problem**: The philosophical problem of how an AI's internal symbols can acquire real-world meaning without physical interaction.
- **Reality Gap**: The significant difference between how a system behaves in a simulated environment versus the real world, due to unmodeled physics, sensor noise, and other factors.
- **Affordance Theory**: The concept that objects in the environment are perceived in terms of the actions they "afford" (e.g., a chair affords sitting, a knob affords turning).
